THE CHANGING FACE OF HIV AIDS

30 years ago a rare immuno-system virus was detected in 5 men in USA. By the following summer, the illness was spreading nationwide and had been given a name: Acquired Immuno Deficiency Syndrome, or AIDS. In the following decades, AIDS has claimed millions of lives around the world. Thankfully, with proper education and advanced medicine, recent trends suggest a decline in AIDS-related death rates. But we've still got a long way left to go.

NEW CASES OF HIV AIDS

Incidence of HIV, measured as the number of new cases per 1000 individuals of all ages, based on UNAIDS versus IHME, Global Burden of Disease estimates.

Death rates are high across Sub-Saharan Africa

In the interactive map, we see the distribution of death rates worldwide. Most countries have a rate of less than 10 deaths per 100,000, often much lower, below 5 per 100,000. Across Europe, the death rate is less than one per 100,000. Across Sub-Saharan Africa, the rates are much higher. Most countries in the South of the region had rates greater than 100 per 100,000.

HIV/AIDS once accounted for a large share of deaths in some countries, but rates are now falling

Today the share of deaths remains high: more than 1 in 5 deaths in some countries are caused by HIV/AIDS. But in the past, this share was even higher.

HIV/AIDS is particulary deadly within younger demographics

Although the death count has declined with time, younger populations, particularly aged within 15 to 49 years are more susceptible to the virus.

Gender and Racial disparity in AIDS deaths in the United States

In the United States, some racial/ethnic groups are more affected than others

This is because some population groups have higher rates of HIV in their communities, thus raising the risk of new infections with each sexual or injection drug use encounter.

More men tend to die from AIDS every year than women

The reasons for differences in death rates include:

  • Starting treatment at later stages of disease progression

  • Worse adherance to treatment follow ups compared to female counterparts
